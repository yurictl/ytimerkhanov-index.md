## Prioritization & Time Management

> "How do you prioritize tasks and manage your time effectively?" / "Describe a situation where you had to work under pressure and how you handled it."

I have a structured approach to my day, and it's built around the timezone I work in — UTC+8, Malaysia.

Europe comes online around midday my time. That means my mornings are quiet. I protect that time for focused, individual work — the kind that requires deep concentration: writing Terraform modules, building a new pipeline, researching a problem, coding a tool. Nobody needs anything from me yet, so I can go deep without interruptions.

After lunch I usually go to the gym. That's deliberate — not just a habit. Physical activity breaks the mental loop. I come back sharper and ready for a different kind of work.

Afternoons and evenings are for the team: meetings, code reviews, syncs, anything that requires other people. European colleagues are fully online and I can work through anything that got blocked in the morning.

For task tracking, I use Obsidian. I capture decisions, open questions, and context notes so I'm not relying on memory. When I pick up work after a weekend or switch between projects, I can quickly reconstruct where I was.

On prioritization specifically: I always ask "what's blocking other people?" before "what do I want to work on?" If something I do unblocks three other people, that goes to the top of the list.

## Staying Current

> "How do you stay current with developments in your field?"

Three ways.

**First: follow the community.** I scan Hacker News, a few infrastructure-focused Slack communities, and some people on LinkedIn who write well about DevOps and AI. I don't read everything — I scan headlines daily and go deep when something is directly relevant to my work.

**Second: certifications for structured learning.** When I need to build solid knowledge in an area, I use certifications as the structure. Not just for the badge — because a well-designed certification forces you to learn things in the right order and fills gaps you didn't know you had.

The CKA is a good example. I needed Kubernetes experience for a project. I prepared for the exam, failed the first attempt, went back and studied harder, then passed with 94 out of 100. That built real confidence — not just surface familiarity.

**Third: I build things.** The AI tools I've made — the documentation generator, the Terraform plan analyzer — weren't required by anyone. I built them because I wanted to understand how LLMs actually work in a real DevOps context. You learn differently when something is deployed and occasionally breaking, even if it's just a small internal tool. That kind of hands-on learning sticks in a way that reading articles doesn't.

## Where Do You See Yourself in N Years

> "Where do you see yourself in 3–5 years?" / "What are your long-term career goals?"

I want to stay on the technical path — I'm not drawn to pure management. What interests me is expanding the scope of my technical influence: not just executing infrastructure work, but shaping how infrastructure decisions get made across teams.

Specifically, I want to get better at the intersection of infrastructure and AI tooling. The tools I've built at EPAM — the Terraform plan analyzer, the documentation generator — showed me that there's a lot of headroom in applying LLMs to DevOps workflows. I want to go deeper there: build more sophisticated tooling, work on multi-cloud architectures, and contribute to platform engineering decisions at a level where my input shapes what the team builds next.

Beyond the technical scope — I care about cross-team communication. Technical problems are rarely purely technical. I want to be the kind of engineer who can translate between infrastructure constraints and product or business needs, not just execute tickets.

Five years from now, I'd like to be a senior individual contributor who others come to for architectural decisions — not just implementation.

# Group 2 — Complex Technical Project

Questions: Significant challenge / Project you're proud of / Working under pressure / Tight deadlines

> One core story. Shift the emphasis depending on the question — the variants below show how.

---

## Core Story

**Project**: IAD platform — EPAM (current role, ~1 year)

**Situation**: The company decided to move away from AppDynamics — our main monitoring tool — because of rising costs. There was a hard end-of-year deadline. But there was no plan. No architecture defined. Nobody on the team had done this transition before.

**Task**: I was asked to lead the POC — figure out what the replacement should look like, prove it works, and build a transition plan. Essentially: start with an empty page and deliver something concrete under a real deadline.

**Action**: I started by mapping what we actually needed — what AppDynamics was doing for us, what we'd lose, and what was critical to keep. Then I reached out to a neighboring team who had already built something with Prometheus and Grafana. Instead of starting from scratch, I learned from their setup, adapted it to our environment, and built the POC on top of that.

While doing the cost analysis for the monitoring piece, I noticed other patterns in our cloud spend that looked expensive. I ran a full analysis — backup policies, idle resources, usage patterns. That wasn't in my task. But it seemed like the right thing to do.

**Result**: The POC was completed and became the foundation of the migration plan. The cost analysis delivered $100,000 in annual savings and reversed the trend — costs had been growing, and after that work they started going down.

## Version: Significant Challenge

> Focus: the uncertainty at the start

*"The hardest part wasn't the technical work — it was the empty page. Nobody had defined what the new system should look like. I had to figure out what 'done' meant, then build toward it.*

*I started by defining the requirements myself, then talked to a neighboring team who had relevant experience. That combination — taking ownership of the problem definition, and not being too proud to ask for help — is what made it work.*

*What I learned: when the task is unclear, the first job is to make it clear. Don't wait for someone to hand you a plan."*

## Version: Working Under Pressure / Tight Deadlines

> Focus: how you managed with a hard deadline and no clear path

*"The pressure came from two directions: a hard end-of-year deadline, and no defined path to get there.*

*What helped was breaking it down early. I identified what the minimum viable POC needed to demonstrate, focused on that, and didn't let perfect get in the way of working.*

*I also made a smart shortcut: I found a neighboring team who had already solved a similar problem. That connection saved weeks of work. Recognizing when not to start from scratch is something I try to do on every project — it's often the most valuable decision you can make under pressure."*
